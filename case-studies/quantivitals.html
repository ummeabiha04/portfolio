31% of transfusions are performed before lab results arrive. This delayed assessment leads to unnecessary transfusions, wastage of donor blood, and increased mortality.

Existing solutions fail where they are needed most: in dark-skinned patients or those with low perfusion.

03 Discover

Clinical Immersion at AIIMS Jodhpur

My discovery phase was rooted in **clinical immersion**. I worked alongside anesthesiologists in high-pressure environments—surgery, trauma, and the ICU—to identify the "unseen" friction in blood management.

Critical Insight: We found that **31% of blood transfusions** were performed reactively because lab tests simply didn't arrive fast enough. This wasn't just a sensor problem; it was an **information-gap problem** that led to higher post-operative mortality and blood wastage.

04 Prioritization

Milestone-Based Capital Allocation

With limited initial funding ($18,000), prioritization was about **Value-to-Risk**. I used the **RICE framework** to ensure every rupee spent bridged the "commercial valley of death" between the lab and the ICU.

High Impact / Low Effort: Oral Probe

Prioritized the Oral Probe for V1 because its "one-time measurement" ritual mimics a thermometer, allowing for zero-training adoption in busy surgeries.

Strategic Choice: ML over Hardware

Instead of seeking expensive, custom sensors, I prioritized investment in the **Machine Learning Pipeline** to clean signals from off-the-shelf components.

05 Design & Develop

Technological Innovation: Sensor Fusion

Our novelty lies in the **machine learning pipeline** that cancels noise, adapts to anatomical sites, and compensates for clinical lubricants (like lignocaine).

We fused data from **Optical, Temperature, Electrical, and Motion sensors** into a common sink. This "multi-parametric" approach is what allowed the device to remain accurate during patient motion and low-perfusion states.

06 Trade-offs

Product Strategy vs. Engineering Idealism

A major product decision was choosing the anatomical sites for the probes. We used a **Risk-Benefit-Adoption Matrix** to evaluate our options.

Option A: Peripheral (Finger)

  • Benefit: High user familiarity; easy access.
  • Risk: 24% failure rate in low perfusion (shock/trauma). High contamination risk.
  • Verdict: REJECTED

Option B: Central (Oral/Esophageal)

  • Benefit: Stable signals, better response times, and resilience to low perfusion.
  • Risk: Higher invasive perception; needs clinical buy-in.
  • Verdict: SELECTED

**The Strategy:** We chose the **Central Site** approach. In critical care, reliability is the only feature that matters. By using existing form factors (esophageal catheters), we bypassed the "invasive perception" hurdle.

08 Critical Analysis

PM Retrospective: Scaling Innovation

The User Acceptance Risk

Feedback identified "User Acceptance" as a Tier-1 risk. We didn't just ignore it; we embedded the device into existing workflows to make it a **"Utility"** rather than a "New Task".

The "Inclusive" Moat

By solving for dark skin and low perfusion, we built a moat that even global incumbents (Masimo) were struggling with, positioning us perfectly for the B2G/National scale-up.
